The Problem

2,000 installations a month. Zero infrastructure.

When I joined The Sleep Company, 2,000 chair installations were happening every month — all managed through WhatsApp messages and Google Sheets.

015 people spent 2–3 hours every morning manually assigning jobs from Excel sheets — figuring out who goes where, one row at a time.

02Technicians received addresses copy-pasted from WhatsApp, navigated on their own, and called from personal numbers — no masking, no record, no privacy.

03Consumers received zero information — no confirmation, no ETA, no way to know when someone would arrive.

04When consumers called support, the agent had nothing. The ticket would travel through multiple hands in the system, each trying to piece together what was happening.

Every single installation generated 3+ inbound support calls.

Every installation cost ₹700. No platform, no tracking, no data.

How I Approached It

I went on 5–10 installations before I touched Figma.

I didn't start with a wireframe. I went to consumer homes with technicians — physically accompanying them on 5–10 installations to understand what was actually hard about their job.

I sat in the ops team's morning meetings and watched them manually assign jobs for 2 hours every day. I mapped every broken thing I saw. Then I interviewed technicians individually — what was hard, what wasted their time, what caused mistakes.

I also ordered and went through the journey myself as a consumer — receiving no communication, not knowing who and when someone would show up at my door, experiencing the whole anxiety first hand.

Then I sat on the other side of those calls. I worked as a support agent — handling inbound queries, watching agents try to piece together job status from disconnected WhatsApp threads, with no visibility and no tools to give the consumer a real answer.

That's when the full cost of zero infrastructure became undeniable.

Design Principle — Mobile App

Designed for someone who might not be a smartphone power-user.

Technicians ranged from 25 to 50 years old — some were power users, some barely used smartphones beyond calls. My brief to myself:

"If a technician has to ask someone how to use it, I've failed."

  • → Progressive disclosure — each screen shows only what's needed at that step, nothing more
  • → Geo-capture is mandatory and automatic — they cannot proceed without GPS lock
  • → Color-coded CTAs — green to proceed, red to flag — no reading required
  • → One-tap call routes through a masked central number — no personal numbers exposed

Beyond the Build

I didn't just ship the product. I owned the launch.

Before launch: training videos for mobile and admin, written SOPs, two sessions each for technician and admin teams, mock runs on launch day. On day one, I sat with the admin team at their computers for a full week — noting every point of friction in real time. The volume of questions, bug reports, and retraining requests that followed was high enough to create a new function. I built and ran a Product Support team from scratch — managed via Jira Service Desk, weekly connects in the first two months, monthly refreshers after. Every feature shipped came with a training session. Every escalation had an owner.

Key Decisions

The calls that defined the outcome.

01

Decision 01

Build vs Buy

  • ▸Market tools: ₹2–3L upfront + per-technician costs that explode at scale
  • ▸No vendor could own our routing logic — our core differentiator

→ Build in-house

02

Decision 02

Radical Transparency

  • ▸Ops team wanted to hide failures — logic: fewer updates = fewer escalations
  • ▸Data showed the opposite: hiding failures generated more repeat calls

→ Real ETAs + real updates

03

Decision 03

The ₹2.5L API Bill

  • ▸25 technicians → ₹2.5L Google Maps bill in one month
  • ▸Root cause: zero caching — same A→B recalculated every call
  • ▸Fix: permanent cache + Haversine fallback + Ola Maps backup

→ ₹0 to 400 technicians

04

Decision 04

3-Tier Prioritisation

  • 1TAT-expiring — committed today, always first
  • 2Premium (₹50k+) — go second
  • 3Future/rescheduled — negotiable, go last
  • ▸3 failures → Needs Intervention, escalated to Support Agent

What I'd Do Differently

Honest reflection.

I'd have instrumented the routing algorithm on day one. We flew blind for the first two months on quality metrics — completion rate by technician, average job duration, first-attempt success rate. We built the reporting eventually, but earlier data would have changed some Phase 1 prioritisation decisions.

I'd also have defined the consumer communication triggers more precisely before launch. We had to iterate on which status changes should fire a WhatsApp message — some triggered too frequently and felt spammy, some not enough. A tighter spec upfront would have saved a month of tuning.

The Product Support function worked, but it shouldn't have taken a high volume of post-launch chaos to create it. The function should have been planned from the start — training content, escalation path, SLA — not built reactively.
